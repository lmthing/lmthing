# Interaction classes the literature commonly notes

These are well-known example pairings worth recognizing when they come up — framed here as
"things the literature commonly notes," never as a standing verdict to apply without actually
researching the specific medication and pairing the user has. Every one of these still needs its
own `review` pass, its own sources, and its own honest `severity` read; this list is orientation,
not a substitute for the research step.

## Grapefruit and certain statins / calcium channel blockers

Grapefruit (and grapefruit juice) inhibits an intestinal enzyme that some statins (notably
simvastatin and, to a lesser degree, atorvastatin) and some calcium channel blockers rely on for
normal metabolism — reputable sources commonly note this can raise blood levels of the drug.
Not every statin or CCB is affected the same way; check the specific medication rather than
assuming the class-wide caution applies uniformly.

## Warfarin and vitamin K / NSAIDs

Warfarin's effect is sensitive to dietary vitamin K intake (found in leafy greens), and reputable
sources commonly note an increased bleeding risk when warfarin is combined with NSAIDs (e.g.
ibuprofen, naproxen). This is one of the most frequently cited drug-food and drug-drug interaction
pairs in pharmacology literature, reflecting a genuinely narrow therapeutic window.

## MAOIs and tyramine-rich foods

Monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs) are commonly noted in the literature to interact with
tyramine-rich foods (aged cheeses, cured meats, some fermented products), with sources describing a
risk of a sharp blood pressure rise. This is a long-standing, well-documented pairing in
pharmacology references.

## SSRIs and other serotonergic agents

Combining an SSRI with another serotonergic drug (certain other antidepressants, some migraine
medications, some supplements like St. John's Wort) is commonly flagged in the literature for an
increased risk of serotonin syndrome. Sources vary on how strongly they weight this depending on the
specific pairing — say so when they do, rather than flattening it to one severity.

## Always research the actual pairing

These are recognizable examples, not a lookup table — the `review` action still searches and cites
sources for the medication and `otherName` actually in front of it, because dose, formulation, and
the user's specific combination can change what applies.

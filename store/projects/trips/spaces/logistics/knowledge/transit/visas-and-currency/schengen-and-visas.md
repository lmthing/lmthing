# Schengen and visa basics (general orientation — not legal advice)

## The Schengen 90/180 rule

The Schengen Area (most of the EU plus a few non-EU countries — Norway, Iceland, Switzerland,
Liechtenstein) treats its members as one space for short-stay purposes: many passports get visa-
free entry, but only for up to 90 days within any rolling 180-day window, counted across *all*
Schengen countries combined, not per country. A traveller who spent 60 days in the Schengen area
earlier in the same rolling window has only 30 days left, regardless of which specific countries
are on the current itinerary. This is the single most common trip-planning surprise for
longer or multi-trip travel, and it's worth flagging explicitly whenever a trip's total days in
Schengen countries (this trip plus any recent prior Schengen travel the traveller mentions)
approaches 90 — the note should ask the traveller to check their own recent Schengen days if the
current trip alone doesn't obviously clear the limit.

## Visa-on-arrival, e-visas, and ETAs

Many countries fall into one of three lighter-weight categories rather than requiring a full
advance visa application: **visa-free** (passport-dependent, sometimes with a maximum stay),
**visa-on-arrival** (pay and get a stamp/sticker at the border, sometimes with an online
pre-registration option that speeds up the line), and **e-visa/ETA schemes** (an online
application, often approved within days, that must be done *before* departure even though it
isn't a full embassy visa — the UK's ETA, the US's ESTA, and a growing number of Southeast Asian
and African e-visa systems all work this way). The critical trap is that an ETA/e-visa still has a
hard "before you travel" deadline and sometimes a processing window of several days to weeks — it
is not the same as visa-on-arrival, and conflating the two is a common and consequential mistake.
When `webSearch` turns up an e-visa/ETA requirement, treat it with the same urgency as a full visa
in the note — flag the "apply before departure" deadline clearly.

## Passport validity and blank-page rules

A near-universal (though not literally universal) requirement is a passport valid for at least six
months beyond the *departure* date from the destination, plus one or two blank visa pages — this
trips up travellers whose passport is technically "not expired" but expires within the trip's
window. Worth a one-line mention in the advisory note when the trip's dates are close to a stated
or guessable passport expiry, though the navigator generally won't know the traveller's actual
passport expiry and should phrase this as a thing to check rather than a finding.

## The caveat, always

None of the above should ever be presented as the final word — entry requirements change with
policy updates, bilateral agreements, and the traveller's specific passport, and a general search
cannot fully capture edge cases (dual nationality, prior visa overstays, criminal record
disclosures). Every visa-related `knowledge_notes` body should close with an explicit line to the
effect of: "This is general orientation, not legal advice — verify current requirements with the
relevant consulate or embassy before booking anything non-refundable."

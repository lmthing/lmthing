# Evaluating worth

## Worth it relative to WHOM

"Is this worth doing" has no answer independent of the traveller. A research report should read the
trip's `brief` for the traveller's stated tastes (food-focused, walking-heavy, museum-averse,
traveling with kids, slow/relaxed vs. fast/see-everything) and weigh recommendations against those,
not against a generic "average tourist." A three-hour modern art museum is a highlight for a
traveller who said "we love contemporary art" and a waste of a half-day for one who said "we mostly
want to eat and walk around neighborhoods." Don't hedge everything to fit everyone — a report that
says "there's something for everyone here" for every destination is not useful; make an actual
judgment call given what's known about this traveller.

## Opportunity cost, not just quality

Nearly every well-known attraction is "good" in isolation — the real question is whether it's good
*relative to the alternative use of that block of time*. A perfectly pleasant but unremarkable
half-day activity crowds out a genuinely special one if the itinerary only has room for one. When
recent sources suggest a place is merely "fine, worth a look if you're already there" rather than
"worth going out of your way for," say that plainly — it lets the scheduler correctly time-box it
(a quick stop, not a dedicated half-day) or drop it in favor of something with a stronger signal.

## Tourist-trap heuristics

Recent traveller reports are the best signal for tourist traps, but some patterns show up
repeatedly and are worth actively searching for:

- **Anything explicitly marketed as "the most Instagrammable spot in X"** — often means heavily
  crowded, roped queues, and a price premium for the same view available a block away.
- **Restaurants directly adjacent to the single most famous landmark** — reliably worse value and
  quality than something two or three blocks away; search for "where locals eat near X" rather than
  trusting proximity.
- **"Skip-the-line" tickets marketed aggressively** — sometimes a genuine time-saver, sometimes a
  markup on a line that isn't actually that long outside peak season; check recent reports for
  actual wait times in the trip's season before recommending the upsell.
- **A single five-star-average attraction with very few recent reviews** — often means the reviews
  are old, incentivized, or not representative of current conditions; weight review recency over
  raw average.

None of these are absolute rules — they're prompts to actually check a recent source rather than
assume, which is the whole point of doing real research instead of listing from memory.

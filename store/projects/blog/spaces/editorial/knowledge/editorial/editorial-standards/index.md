# Editorial standards

Every reader-facing sentence the editorial desk produces — a synthesized article, a digest blurb, a
newsletter intro, an editor's note pinned to an article card — carries the same implicit promise: it
is a fair, sourced, honestly-framed account of something that actually happened, written by someone
who read the underlying material rather than someone who guessed what it probably said. The
newsroom's synthesizer already holds this standard for the article body itself; the editorial desk
(curator, digest-writer, personalizer) holds it for everything built on top of that body — selection,
framing, and the words added around an article rather than in it.

Two habits carry almost all of this standard in practice:

1. **Say no more than you actually know.** A blurb, a subject line, or an editor's note should never
   claim more certainty, more novelty, or more importance than the underlying article supports. This
   is expanded in `voice-and-tone.md` — the house voice is measured and specific, not hyped.

2. **Never disconnect a claim from its source.** Every article already carries its `citations` back
   to the raw item it was synthesized from; the editorial desk's job is to preserve that chain, not
   sever it — a digest blurb summarizing "why this matters" should be defensible by pointing back at
   the article it's attached to, never embellished with a detail the article itself doesn't contain.
   This is expanded in `accuracy-and-provenance.md`, along with what to do when something later turns
   out to be wrong.

A curator, digest-writer, or personalizer that holds to both of these will never need to walk
anything back — which is the actual goal, since none of these agents have a correction workflow of
their own beyond simply not getting it wrong in the first place.

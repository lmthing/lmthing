# Research method

The Analyst is the research space's general-purpose investigator: given a topic — a reader's free
question in chat, or a request to dig into something the feed has only touched briefly — it produces
a briefing that goes beyond any single article. That's a genuinely different job from summarizing one
raw item. It means actively deciding what the real question is, pulling from more than one place
(the open web via search/fetch, and the app's own feed and collections via its data tools), and then
writing up a synthesis that a reader can trust because every claim in it is traceable to something
actually gathered during the session.

Two disciplines make up good research method here:

1. **Framing the question well before gathering anything.** A vague topic ("AI regulation", "what's
   going on with X") is not yet a research question — it has to be scoped into something answerable,
   and the right scope depends on what the reader actually wants to know: what happened, why it
   matters, or what's likely next are three different briefings, not one. `framing-a-question.md`
   covers how to do that decomposition, and when a topic is really several questions wearing a
   trenchcoat.

2. **Synthesizing across sources without inventing anything.** Once material is gathered — fetched
   web pages and/or matching articles already in the feed — it has to be reconciled (sources agree,
   partially agree, or flatly disagree) and structured into a briefing that reads as a briefing, not
   a stitched-together pile of excerpts. `multi-source-synthesis.md` covers that structure, how to
   weigh sources against each other, and the non-negotiable rule that nothing goes into a briefing
   unless it traces back to a fetched page or a real feed article.

The Analyst's real-time access to the web and the feed is not a shortcut to sound authoritative — it
exists so that what gets written down reflects what was actually found this session, honestly,
including when what was found is thin.

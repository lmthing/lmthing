# Reading a forecast into a packing list

## Temperature range, not a single number

A destination's forecast is a range, and the range matters more than the average — a daily high of
22°C with a low of 9°C calls for very different packing than a steady 22°C day and night. When
`webSearch` turns up a typical high/low for the trip's dates, use both ends: the high drives
daytime clothing (short sleeves, breathable fabrics), the low drives what's needed for evenings
and mornings (a mid-layer, a light jacket). A wide daily swing is itself a packing signal —
layering becomes the strategy rather than picking one "right" outfit weight.

## Layering logic

The practical answer to a variable forecast is layers rather than a single heavy or light
wardrobe: a base layer suited to the daytime high, a mid-layer (fleece, light sweater) for the
morning/evening lows, and a weather shell (rain jacket, windbreaker) sized to fit over both when
precipitation or wind is in the forecast. This beats packing one bulky "average" garment because it
adapts across the swing instead of being wrong at one end of it. When the forecast shows a big
day/night gap (see `shoulder-season-traps.md`), the packing list should include an explicit
mid-layer item rather than assuming the traveller's daytime clothing covers the evening too.

## Precipitation — when to act on it

Not every rain chance justifies a dedicated item. As a rough threshold: a searched forecast
showing rain likely on 2+ days of the trip, or a destination's month having a well-documented wet
season, justifies a rain jacket and/or packable umbrella as an explicit item. An isolated "30%
chance" on one day of a otherwise dry-forecast trip is a weaker signal — worth a note rather than a
dedicated purchase-grade item, since most itineraries have enough flexibility to duck into
somewhere covered for a passing shower. Humidity is a related but distinct signal: a hot, humid
forecast (much of Southeast Asia, the US Gulf Coast in summer) favors breathable/quick-dry fabrics
over cotton, which stays wet and uncomfortable — worth calling out specifically when the search
turns up high humidity alongside heat.

## Footwear from the forecast

Wet-weather forecasts and the itinerary's activities jointly determine footwear more than either
alone: a rainy-season destination with mostly city walking calls for water-resistant everyday
shoes, not full hiking boots; a dry-season destination with a hiking day calls for boots regardless
of rain chance. Don't default to "bring boots just in case" — tie the specific footwear item to
either a specific wet forecast or a specific activity (see `activity-to-gear-mapping.md`), and skip
it when neither applies.

## Sun and heat signals

A forecast showing consistently high UV or extended sun exposure (a beach destination, a
desert climate, high altitude where UV is stronger) justifies sun-specific items — a wide-brim hat,
higher-SPF sunscreen, sunglasses — as concretely as a rain forecast justifies a rain jacket. High
altitude in particular is worth flagging even when the temperature reading looks mild, since UV
exposure rises independent of air temperature.

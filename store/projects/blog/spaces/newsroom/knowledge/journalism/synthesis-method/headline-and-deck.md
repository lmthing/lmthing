# Headline and deck

## The `title` is a claim, not a hook

An article's `title` is the single piece of text a reader sees the most, across the feed list, the
digest, and any share. The single most important property a headline can have is that it be *true
on its own* — a reader who reads only the title and never opens the article should not come away
with a wrong or exaggerated impression of what's inside. This rules out two common failure modes:
question headlines that imply an answer the article doesn't actually give ("Is X about to change
everything?" when the raw item is a minor incremental update), and headlines that state the most
extreme possible reading of an ambiguous raw item rather than the most accurate one. A good
headline states the actual news plainly — specific enough that two different stories in the same
topic area would get two genuinely different, informative headlines, not two generic ones
("New AI Model Released" tells a reader nothing that ten other headlines that week wouldn't also
say).

## Clickbait costs trust, and trust is the whole product

A newsroom that personalizes and curates a feed for one reader has exactly one thing to sell: that
reader's confidence that what's in the feed is worth their time and honestly represented. A single
headline that overpromises relative to its article is cheap to write and expensive to the product —
once a reader catches one exaggerated headline, they start reading every headline skeptically,
which defeats the purpose of curation. Concretely, avoid: superlatives not supported by the excerpt
("the biggest," "game-changing," "shocking") unless the raw item itself makes that case with
specifics; withholding the actual subject of the headline to force a click ("This one change could
affect millions" — say what the change is); and stacking multiple vague intensifiers instead of one
concrete fact.

## The `summary` (deck) does different work than the title

`summary` is the one-paragraph deck shown under the title in the feed list — its job is to let a
reader accurately decide whether to open the article, not to re-hook them with more intrigue. A
good deck states the two or three most important pieces of information the article contains (what
happened, who's involved, why it matters) in plain declarative sentences. It should never simply
restate the title in slightly different words — that wastes the reader's scan time — and it should
never introduce a detail that isn't also substantiated in the body. Think of the deck as the honest
answer to "what would you tell a friend who asked what this article says, in one breath" — not as a
second, longer headline competing for the same attention the title already earned.

# Value vs. price

## A lower headline number isn't automatically a better deal

The easiest mistake in deal-hunting is treating "cheaper" as the whole story. A discounted hotel
rate that turns out to be for a property far outside the destinations the trip actually plans to
spend time in isn't a saving — it trades money for time and convenience the traveller didn't ask to
give up. A city pass priced below the sum of individual admissions is only a real saving if the
trip's actual planned activities use enough of what's included; a pass covering a dozen attractions
the itinerary has no interest in visiting is not a deal just because the headline math looks
favorable in the abstract. Always check a saving against the trip's *actual* destinations,
itinerary, and dates before writing it — a deal has to be relevant to this trip, not just cheap in
general.

## Reading the fine print that makes a discount hollow

Discounts on transit passes, activity bundles, and some fares often come with restrictions that
matter more than the headline percentage off: blackout dates that overlap the trip's actual travel
window, a non-refundable condition on an itinerary that still has real uncertainty in it, a
"starting from" price that only applies to a specific date/time the traveller isn't actually
planning to use, or a bundle that requires purchasing add-ons the traveller doesn't want. When a
search turns up a promising headline price, a second pass checking for these conditions is what
separates a deal worth writing from one that looks good only until someone tries to book it.

## Real savings that don't look like discounts

Some of the best savings the deal-hunter can surface aren't framed as "deals" at all in the source
material — a transit pass that's cheaper than the sum of individual fares for the number of rides
the itinerary actually implies, an off-peak time slot for an activity that costs the same but avoids
the worst crowds (a value gain even without a price gain), or simply noting that a destination's
shoulder season would cut lodging costs substantially with a small, named tradeoff. These require
connecting a searched fact to the trip's specific plans rather than pattern-matching on the word
"discount," but they're often more useful to the traveller than a marginal percentage off a fare
they'd have paid anyway.

## When a "deal" doesn't survive scrutiny

If a search result looks like it's mostly marketing (an inflated "was" price with no evidence
anyone actually paid it, a countdown timer clearly designed to manufacture urgency, an affiliate
page recycling the same "top 10 deals" copy across many destinations), don't write it as a `deals`
row just because a number was mentioned. The bar is "would this hold up if the traveller actually
tried to book it right now" — if that's doubtful, it isn't a deal yet, and writing nothing is the
honest outcome.

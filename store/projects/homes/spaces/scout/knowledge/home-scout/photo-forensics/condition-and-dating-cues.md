# Condition and dating cues

## Language that dates a space

Certain caption/description phrasing reliably signals age or renovation state even without seeing
anything: a named fixture or material era ("original parquet," "1970s tile," "avocado bathroom
suite" — yes, this is a real and recognizable idiom), an explicit renovation claim with or without a
year ("renovated 2019" vs. bare "renovated" — the latter is vaguer and worth treating with lower
confidence about HOW recent), and appliance-brand or style mentions that skew toward a decade
("laminate countertops" reads differently than "quartz countertops" as a dating signal, though
neither is proof on its own).

## Contradiction is the strongest signal, not any single caption

A single caption naming an old material is a mild, single-source observation. The MISMATCH worth
flagging is when the description makes a broad positive age claim ("fully renovated throughout") that
a specific caption or room-by-room caption set doesn't support ("original tile" on the bathroom
photo, while the living room is captioned "new flooring") — that's a `mismatch`-kind finding, not a
`photos`-kind one, because it's a contradiction between two stated things, not a read of one thing in
isolation.

## Confidence calibration for condition reads

A `photos` analysis about condition should rarely claim high confidence — text-only condition
assessment is inherently a soft read. Reserve higher confidence (0.6–0.7) for cases with multiple
independent captions agreeing, and keep single-caption or purely descriptive-adjective-based reads
(a listing simply saying "cozy" or "charming" — marketing language, not a dating cue) at low
confidence or skip them: adjectives like these carry near-zero forensic value and shouldn't anchor a
finding at all. Phrase anything below ~0.5 confidence as a question ("worth checking the kitchen's
actual age at a viewing") rather than a stated fact.

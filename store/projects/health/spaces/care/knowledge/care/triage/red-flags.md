# Red flags

The patterns below generally warrant **urgent or emergency** framing when they appear in a
`triage_assessments` question or its linked symptom — always phrased as "consider seeking care" or
"call emergency services," never as a diagnosis, a probability, or a specific instruction beyond
seeking care. This list is deliberately general — general education about *when to seek care*, not
a diagnostic tool.

## Cardiac / vascular

- Chest pain or pressure, especially with exertion, or radiating to the arm, jaw, neck, or back.
- Chest pain or pressure accompanied by shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, or lightheadedness.
- Fainting or near-fainting, especially sudden or recurrent.

## Neurological — think FAST for stroke

- Sudden one-sided weakness or numbness (face, arm, or leg).
- Facial drooping, especially sudden and one-sided.
- Sudden difficulty speaking, slurred speech, or trouble understanding speech.
- Sudden severe headache unlike any before ("worst headache of my life"), especially with
  confusion, vision change, stiff neck, or trouble speaking.
- Sudden vision loss or double vision.

_(FAST — **F**ace drooping, **A**rm weakness, **S**peech difficulty, **T**ime to call emergency
services — is a widely used public memory aid for stroke, not a diagnostic claim this app makes;
it's how the escalation line should be phrased when this pattern appears.)_

## Respiratory

- Severe or sudden shortness of breath, at rest or with minimal exertion.
- Difficulty breathing that is worsening, or breathing that requires visibly increased effort.
- Signs of anaphylaxis — sudden swelling of the face/throat/tongue, widespread hives with
  difficulty breathing, or a known severe-allergy trigger with rapid symptom onset.

## Abdominal / gastrointestinal

- Severe, unrelenting abdominal pain, especially sudden onset or with a rigid/board-like abdomen.
- Abdominal pain with persistent vomiting, inability to keep fluids down, or signs of dehydration.

## Fever / infection

- High fever together with a stiff neck, severe headache, confusion, or a rash that doesn't fade
  under pressure.
- A fever in a person who is immunocompromised, or a fever in a very young infant — both warrant a
  lower threshold for prompt attention than the general population.

## Mental health

- Suicidal ideation, a stated plan or intent to self-harm, or a mental-health crisis — always
  emergency framing. Provide the escalation line plainly and without judgment; this is exactly the
  kind of case where hesitation to escalate can cost the most.

## Severity as a standalone signal

- A symptom logged at high severity (4-5 out of 5) that is **new or rapidly worsening**, rather
  than a known, previously-discussed, stable pattern, generally warrants at least an "urgent"
  framing even without a named red-flag pattern above — severity plus novelty is itself a signal.

## How to phrase it

"This combination — [symptom/pattern] — is the kind of thing that's generally worth prompt medical
attention. Please consider contacting your clinician or seeking care soon" for `urgent`, or "please
seek care now / call emergency services" for `emergency`. Never assign a specific likelihood, never
name a specific condition, and never tell the user what to do beyond seeking the appropriate level
of care — the triage-nurse's role stops at flagging the pattern and pointing toward care.

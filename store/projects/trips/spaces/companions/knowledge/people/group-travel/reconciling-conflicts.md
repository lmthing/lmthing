# Reconciling conflicting preferences

## Name the conflict, don't silently resolve it

When two travelers' preferences genuinely conflict — a vegetarian and someone whose stated interest
is "trying as much local meat/seafood as possible," a traveler who wants an early-morning-heavy
itinerary and one whose recorded pace preference is "slow mornings, no early starts" — the host's
job is to surface both sides plainly in the party note, not to quietly pick a winner. A note that
says "the party includes both a vegetarian traveler and one interested in local meat/seafood
specialties — the planner should look for venues offering both" is useful; a note that mentions only
the meat-focused interest and drops the vegetarian constraint (or vice versa) actively misinforms
whoever reads it next, and could lead the planner to book something that genuinely doesn't work for
part of the party.

## Hard constraints don't get overridden by a majority

If three travelers on a five-person trip want a strenuous full-day hike and one has a hard
mobility constraint (`weight: 1`) that rules it out, the note doesn't average these into "the group
is mostly fine with a hike." A hard constraint stands regardless of how many other travelers don't
share it — the honest framing is "a full-day hike isn't compatible with [traveler]'s mobility
constraint; consider a shorter or lower-intensity alternative, or splitting the group for that day
if everyone's comfortable with that." Majority preference can legitimately shape *soft* preferences
(pace, interests) but never overrides a stated hard constraint.

## Splitting the group is a legitimate resolution to surface

Not every conflict needs a single itinerary that satisfies everyone simultaneously — sometimes the
honest, useful thing to note is that the party might split for part of a day (some do the hike,
others do something lower-key nearby) rather than forcing one compromise activity that half-satisfies
everyone. The host doesn't need to plan the split itself (that's the scheduler/planner's job
elsewhere in the app) but naming it as an option in the note, when the underlying preferences make
it the natural resolution, is more useful than staying silent about the tension.

## Don't manufacture a conflict that isn't there

Not every difference in preference is a conflict worth flagging prominently — two travelers with
different but compatible interests ("loves museums" and "loves local food") aren't in tension at
all; the itinerary can serve both. Reserve the explicit "these preferences conflict" framing for
cases where satisfying one preference genuinely makes the other harder to satisfy, not for every
pair of travelers who simply like different things.

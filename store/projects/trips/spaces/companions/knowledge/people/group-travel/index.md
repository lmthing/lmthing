# Group travel dynamics

Planning for one traveler is a matter of reading their preferences and respecting them. Planning
for a party is harder, because a group's individual preferences don't always point the same
direction — one traveler wants a packed sightseeing schedule, another wants unstructured downtime;
one is vegetarian, another wants to try every local specialty; one has a hard mobility limit, the
rest of the party would happily do a long hike. The host's "Party preferences & constraints" note is
where these individual signals become one collective picture the planner and packer can actually
use, and how well that reconciliation is done determines whether the resulting trip genuinely works
for everyone or quietly favors whoever's preferences happened to be recorded most vocally.

`reconciling-conflicts.md` covers the mechanics of handling preferences that actively conflict — the
key discipline being to *name* a conflict rather than silently resolve it by picking one side, since
silently resolving it is itself a decision the host has no authority to make unilaterally.
`decision-and-consensus.md` covers the softer judgment calls around whose preferences should weigh
more in a given moment (a hard constraint vs. a soft one, a majority preference vs. a strongly-felt
minority one) and how the note should present those tradeoffs so a human — the traveller or planner
reading it — makes the actual call.

Throughout, the host's own authority is narrow: it records and reconciles what travelers have
actually told the trip, and it flags tension plainly. It never invents a compromise nobody proposed,
and it never quietly drops a stated preference because honoring it would be inconvenient for the
rest of the party.

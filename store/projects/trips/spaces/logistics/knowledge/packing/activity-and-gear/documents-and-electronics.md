# Documents and electronics essentials

## Documents, physical and digital

Regardless of destination, a trip involving international travel justifies a documents item
covering: the physical passport, a photocopy or photo of the passport's ID page stored separately
from the original (in case of loss/theft), printed or offline copies of key confirmations
(accommodation, any visa/e-visa approval, travel insurance), and — when a visa-currency advisory
knowledge_note flagged an e-visa/ETA requirement — the approval confirmation itself, since some
border checks still ask for a printed copy despite the process being digital. This is one of the
few packing categories that's near-universal rather than destination- or activity-triggered, so it
belongs on essentially every trip's list rather than needing a specific itinerary trigger — but the
`reason` should still be concrete ("international travel — passport photocopy in case of loss")
rather than a bare label.

## Power and connectivity

The specific adapter needed is destination-driven (a UK/Ireland-style plug, a Europlug, a
US-style plug, etc. depending on the destination's socket standard, which is worth a quick
`webSearch` rather than assumption since some regions mix standards) — a universal adapter covers
most cases when multiple destinations use different standards within one trip. A portable power
bank is worth adding when the itinerary shows long transit days or full days away from lodging
(reduces the risk of a dead phone during navigation), and is a lower priority for a trip with
frequent returns to a home base. Voltage itself (110V vs 220-240V) rarely needs a separate
converter for modern electronics (phones, laptops, cameras) since most are dual-voltage — only
flag a voltage converter for a specific device known to be single-voltage (some hair
dryers/appliances), which is a narrow enough case to skip unless the traveller's brief mentions
such a device.

## Offline access

A trip with multi-day stretches away from reliable data (rural stretches, international roaming
gaps, a destination with unreliable connectivity found via search) justifies offline-first
preparation: downloaded offline maps for the destinations (most major map apps support this),
saved/downloaded copies of tickets and boarding passes rather than relying on email access, and a
downloaded translation pack if the destination's language isn't one the traveller reads. This is
worth flagging more heavily for multi-destination trips with transit between them than for a
single-city stay with continuous connectivity.

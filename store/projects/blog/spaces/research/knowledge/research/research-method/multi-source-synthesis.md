# Multi-source synthesis

## Two pools of material

The Analyst draws from two distinct pools, and both should be checked before writing anything up:
pages fetched live from the open web (via search/fetch), and articles that already exist in the
app's own feed and collections. The feed pool matters because it's often already been through a
synthesis and fact-checking pass — an existing article on the topic is a legitimate, citable source
in its own right, not just a shortcut to skip research. A good briefing frequently draws on both:
the feed for what the app's own pipeline already established, the open web for anything more recent
or not yet covered.

## Reconciling agreement and disagreement

Once material is gathered, sources rarely all say exactly the same thing, and the reconciliation
work is where synthesis actually happens:

- When sources agree, that's corroboration worth noting briefly, not repeating three times.
- When sources partially agree — same facts, different emphasis or interpretation — the briefing
  should surface the shared factual core and then note where interpretations diverge, attributing
  each interpretation to its source rather than picking one silently.
- When sources flatly disagree, that disagreement is itself a finding. Report it as such — "sources
  differ on X" — rather than either burying it or arbitrarily picking a side without a reason grounded
  in source credibility or recency.

Weight sources by two things: **credibility** (a primary source or a specialist outlet ordinarily
outweighs a secondary aggregator repeating someone else's reporting) and **recency** (a more recent
source supersedes an older one on anything that could have changed, though not on settled historical
fact). Neither factor alone is decisive — a recent low-credibility post doesn't outweigh a slightly
older report from the primary source.

## Structuring the briefing

A useful shape: **lede** (the one or two sentences that answer the core question up front, so a
reader who stops there still learned the answer), **key findings** (the handful of substantive points
the research actually turned up, each grounded), **nuance and caveats** (disagreement among sources,
things that are still unsettled, limits of what was found), and **what to watch** (concrete next
developments worth tracking, when the question has a forward-looking component). Not every briefing
needs all four sections in full — a narrow factual question may not need a "what to watch" — but the
lede-first structure should hold throughout: never bury the answer under a pile of background.

## The hard rule, and reporting thin evidence honestly

Every claim in the briefing must trace to a page that was actually fetched this session or a real
article that actually exists in the feed — never to what the model already believed about the topic
generically. If a search comes back thin, or a sub-question the reader clearly cares about turns up
nothing solid, say so plainly in the briefing ("available sources don't clearly establish X") rather
than quietly smoothing over the gap with plausible-sounding filler. A reader trusts a briefing
precisely because it's presented as sourced; spending that trust on invented certainty costs far more
than an honest "the evidence here is thin."

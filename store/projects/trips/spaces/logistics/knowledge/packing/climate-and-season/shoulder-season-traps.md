# Shoulder-season traps

## Warm by day, cold at night

The most common seasonal surprise is a destination that reads as simply "warm" from its average
temperature but swings sharply after sunset — high-altitude and desert-adjacent destinations
especially (Marrakech, much of the Andes, many Mediterranean hill towns in shoulder months) can
run 20°C+ during the day and drop into single digits at night. A traveller packing only for the
daytime high based on a one-number mental model of "it's warm there" will be genuinely
underprepared for evenings. When a searched forecast shows a large day/night spread, call it out
explicitly as its own packing driver (a warm layer, closed shoes for the evening) rather than
letting it get absorbed into a generic "pack layers" note.

## Rainy seasons that don't match home-climate intuition

Many destinations have a rainy season that falls in a month a traveller from a different climate
wouldn't expect — Southeast Asia's monsoon patterns vary significantly by country and even by
coast within a country (the same month can be dry season on one side and wet season on the other),
and destinations with a Mediterranean climate get most of their annual rain in what a
temperate-climate traveller would consider late autumn/winter, not summer. `webSearch` for the
specific destination and month rather than assuming a "summer = dry" or "winter = wet" pattern
that may not hold locally — this is exactly the kind of assumption that produces an underprepared
traveller in a destination the packer never actually checked.

## "Shoulder season" itself is a signal

Trips booked in shoulder months (roughly April-May and September-October in much of the northern
hemisphere's popular destinations) are disproportionately likely to hit unstable, harder-to-predict
weather compared to a peak-season trip — the season is transitioning, and a forecast search this
far out is more of an outlook than a firm prediction. When the trip's dates fall in a shoulder
month, it's worth erring toward the more conservative (better-prepared) packing choice on
borderline calls — bringing a rain shell even when the outlook leans dry, for instance — rather
than optimizing the list tightly around a single forecast read that's inherently less stable this
far out.

## Coastal vs. inland divergence

Within a single destination, a coastal area and an inland area even a short distance apart can run
noticeably different climates in the same week — a coastal city moderated by sea breeze, paired
with a mountain or interior day-trip that runs colder or wetter than the base city's forecast
suggests. When a trip's itinerary includes a day trip or a destination change between coastal and
inland areas, search each leg's climate separately rather than assuming the base destination's
forecast covers the whole itinerary.

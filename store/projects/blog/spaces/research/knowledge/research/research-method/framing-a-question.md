# Framing a question

## From vague topic to answerable question

A reader asking about "the new tariff announcement" or "what's happening with the drought" hasn't
actually asked a research question yet — they've named a subject. The Analyst's first job, before
any search or fetch happens, is to turn that subject into a specific question that a briefing can
actually answer and a reader can tell has been answered. "What's going on with X" is not answerable
in any checkable way; "what did the announcement actually change, who is affected, and what happens
next" is three answerable sub-questions bundled into one topic, and each deserves its own place in
the eventual briefing rather than being blurred together.

## Three different shapes of question

Most research requests decompose into some mix of three distinct question shapes, and it's worth
naming which one (or ones) the reader actually wants before gathering anything:

- **What happened** — the factual record: what occurred, when, to whom, according to which sources.
  This is the floor every briefing needs, but it's rarely the whole ask.
- **Why it matters** — the significance layer: why this is a bigger deal than the raw facts suggest,
  who is affected and how, what changed relative to the status quo before it.
- **What's next** — the forward-looking layer: what to watch for, what's still unresolved, what
  reasonable observers expect to happen or are watching closely.

A reader who asks a pointed question ("will this affect my mortgage rate") is really asking for the
"why it matters" and "what's next" layers built on top of a "what happened" foundation — briefing all
three, but weighted toward the two that were actually asked for, is better than an evenly-balanced
report that under-serves the actual question.

## Recognizing when a question is too broad

Some topics are genuinely several separate research questions wearing one label — "the state of the
AI industry" is not one question, it's dozens. The honest move when a topic is this broad is to
decompose it explicitly rather than attempt a shallow pass over everything: pick the two or three
sub-questions that most plausibly match what the reader is after (informed by any context — the
article that prompted the request, the reader's recent reading, the specific phrasing used), state
the scoping decision briefly in the briefing itself ("this covers X and Y; it doesn't attempt Z"), and
research those well rather than researching everything shallowly. A narrower, well-sourced briefing
beats a broad one that's thin everywhere.

# Follow-through

## Space follow-ups out, don't stack them

The `checkin` action's default follow-up window is 7 days — long enough for a real trend to show up
in the next rolling average, short enough that the goal doesn't fall off the user's radar entirely.
Because `checkin` skips proposing a new follow-up when one is already open for the same goal, a
slipping goal naturally gets one gentle nudge at a time rather than a pile-up of reminders that all
say the same thing — resist any urge to shorten that window "to be safe"; more frequent nagging
reads as pressure, not support.

## Celebrate small wins as plainly as slips

A goal crossing into `status: 'met'` deserves the same plain, warm acknowledgment as a slip deserves
a gentle nudge — don't only ever surface the coach's voice when something needs fixing. Progress
that's real but short of the target (a 10% improvement toward a 20% goal) is still worth naming in
conversation, even though it doesn't yet warrant flipping the goal to `met`.

## When to propose a recheck instead of a nudge

If a goal has been open a long time with `current` barely moving despite several follow-ups already
having come and gone, the more useful move is often to revisit the target itself rather than send yet
another reminder — the target may have been unrealistic from the start (see `goal-setting.md`), or
the user's circumstances may have changed. Say this plainly rather than quietly lowering the bar: "this
target hasn't budged in a while — want to adjust it, or is something getting in the way?"

## Tone

Every follow-up and every check-in message should read as something a supportive friend who happens
to track your numbers would say, never as a compliance system flagging a miss. No guilt, no
alarmist language, no comparison to what "most people" achieve. And always: this is coaching and
encouragement, not medical advice — a goal that touches on a symptom or a lab value belongs in
conversation with the user's own clinician, not a target the coach adjusts unilaterally.

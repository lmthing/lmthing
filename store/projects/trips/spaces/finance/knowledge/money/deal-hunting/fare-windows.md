# Fare and booking windows

## Flights

Short-haul fares typically bottom out somewhere in the 4-8-week-before-departure range; long-haul
fares often show their best pricing 2-3 months out. Prices tend to climb steeply inside the final
2-3 weeks, especially on routes with limited daily frequency where a airline can price more
aggressively against a captive near-term demand. Day-of-week effects are real but smaller than
booking-window effects — searching and booking mid-week is sometimes marginally cheaper than a
weekend, but it's a minor lever compared to booking far enough ahead. When the deal-hunter finds a
specific fare-calendar tool or a cited pattern for the exact route in question, that beats a generic
rule of thumb — cite it and note the actual dates/prices found.

## Hotels and short-term rentals

Unlike flights, hotel pricing doesn't reliably get cheaper with more lead time — it's driven more
by occupancy and local demand than by a fixed booking curve. What *is* usually true: booking
directly with a property (rather than through a third-party aggregator) sometimes unlocks a
best-rate-guarantee or a small perk (free breakfast, late checkout) even at the same headline price,
and many properties offer meaningfully lower nightly rates for a longer stay (a week or more) than
the same nights booked as separate shorter stays. Shoulder-season travel (just before or after a
destination's peak) can cut accommodation costs substantially with only a modest tradeoff in
weather or crowd levels — worth surfacing explicitly if the trip's dates are flexible or if visiting
slightly earlier/later would still fit the traveller's plans.

## Activities and city passes

Multi-attraction city passes (a "Lisboa Card," a "Paris Museum Pass," and similar) only save money
if the traveller's actual planned itinerary uses enough of the included attractions to clear the
pass's break-even point — searching the pass's price against the sum of the individual admission
prices for what's *actually on this trip's itinerary* is the right comparison, not a generic "this
pass covers 20 attractions" pitch. Time-of-day and day-of-week discounts (many museums offer a
cheaper or free evening/first-Sunday slot) are worth surfacing when found, especially for
budget-conscious trips, alongside a note about any tradeoff (more crowded, shorter hours).

## When to surface a window vs. when to stay quiet

Not every route or stay has a meaningfully exploitable booking-window pattern — a short regional
train with flat, unreserved pricing has essentially no window worth naming. Surface a window only
when the search actually turned up a concrete, citable pattern for the route/stay/activity in
question; a generic "book early to save" reminder with nothing route-specific behind it isn't worth
a `deals` row.

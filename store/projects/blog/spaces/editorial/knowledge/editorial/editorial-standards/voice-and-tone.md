# Voice and tone

## The house voice

lmthing.blog's house voice is that of a well-read, unhurried colleague explaining why something is
worth your attention — not a headline-writer competing for a click, and not a press release
restating what a source already claimed about itself. Concretely, that means:

- **Specific over generic.** "A new benchmark suggests X model narrows the gap on long-context
  reasoning" beats "Exciting new AI breakthrough!" every time. If a blurb or subject line could be
  reused verbatim for a dozen unrelated articles, it hasn't said anything yet.
- **Measured over hyped.** Words like "revolutionary," "game-changing," or "unprecedented" are almost
  never earned by a single article — reach for them only when the underlying source itself makes and
  supports that exact claim, and even then, prefer describing the specific claim over repeating the
  adjective.
- **Plain over jargon-stuffed.** Technical precision matters more than sounding technical — write the
  way a knowledgeable friend would explain it to someone who's smart but hasn't been following this
  particular story.

## Honest framing

Framing is where most editorial dishonesty creeps in without anyone writing a single false sentence.
A blurb that only ever mentions upside, that presents a preliminary result as settled fact, or that
picks the most dramatic possible reading of an ambiguous finding, is technically-true but misleading
— which is its own kind of inaccuracy. When a source is preliminary, early-stage, disputed, or
one-sided, say so in the framing rather than smoothing it into a confident declarative sentence: "an
early report claims" reads differently than "X happened," and the difference matters.

## Never overstating

Overstating shows up in small, easy-to-miss places: turning "the team reported improvements on some
benchmarks" into "the team's model now beats everything else," or turning "one researcher's opinion"
into "experts agree." Before finalizing any blurb, subject line, or note, ask: does every word of
this have a direct anchor in the article/source it's describing, or did something get rounded up
along the way? If a claim can't be traced to a specific sentence in the source material, cut it or
soften it rather than let it stand.

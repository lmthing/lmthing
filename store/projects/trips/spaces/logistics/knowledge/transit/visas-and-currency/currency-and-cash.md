# Currency, cash, and tipping norms

## Cash-vs-card by region

Card acceptance varies far more by region than most travellers expect. Most of Western/Northern
Europe, East Asia's major cities, Australia, and North America are heavily card- and
tap-to-pay-first — carrying more than a small amount of cash is often unnecessary outside markets
and very small vendors. Much of Southern/Eastern Europe, Latin America, and large parts of
Southeast Asia and Africa still run substantially on cash for smaller vendors, taxis, tipping, and
rural areas, even where cards work fine in hotels and larger restaurants. When `webSearch` doesn't
clarify a specific destination, the safer default is to note that a small amount of local cash is
worth having on arrival regardless of the country's general card-friendliness, since arrival
logistics (an airport taxi, a tip, a vending machine) are disproportionately likely to be cash-only
even in otherwise card-first places.

## ATM and exchange traps

Airport currency exchange counters and hotel-lobby exchange desks routinely offer meaningfully
worse rates than a bank-affiliated ATM in town — as a rule of thumb, exchanging only enough for
the taxi/first meal at the airport and withdrawing the rest from an ATM later is more favorable.
The recurring ATM trap is **dynamic currency conversion (DCC)**: many overseas ATMs and card
terminals ask "would you like this charged in your home currency instead?" — accepting this
routes the conversion through the *machine's* rate, which is reliably worse than letting your own
bank/card network do the conversion. The correct answer is almost always to decline DCC and pay in
the local currency. Some ATM networks also charge a flat withdrawal fee plus a percentage on top —
worth a `webSearch` for "best ATM to use in [city]" since specific banks (e.g. certain
no-foreign-fee networks) are well-documented per country and can materially reduce total fees
across a trip with several withdrawals.

## Tipping conventions

Tipping norms vary from "expected and calculated into service quality" (much of the US) to
"unusual and can even seem awkward" (Japan, much of East Asia) to "a modest rounding-up gesture"
(much of Europe) — getting this wrong in either direction (stiffing an expectant server, or
insistently tipping in a culture where it reads as strange) is a small but real social friction
point. When writing the advisory, name the specific convention found for each destination country
rather than defaulting to a home-country assumption, and note where a service charge is already
included in the bill (common in parts of Europe and Latin America) versus where it isn't.

## Practical prep before departure

Two habits are worth surfacing regardless of destination: notify the card issuer of travel dates
and destinations to avoid a fraud-flag decline at the worst moment, and carry at least one backup
payment method (a second card, a modest cash reserve) in case a primary card is lost, blocked, or
simply not accepted somewhere unexpected.

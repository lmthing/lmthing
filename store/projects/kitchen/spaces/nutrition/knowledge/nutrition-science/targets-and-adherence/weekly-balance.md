# Weekly balance

## Look at the week, not just the day

A single day being under or over target is rarely meaningful on its own — a lighter lunch before a
big dinner out, or a heavier meal after a long day, is completely ordinary. What's actually worth
noticing is a **pattern across the week**: several days in a row running the same direction, or a
week where every dinner shares the same gap (consistently protein-light, or consistently far over
on calories). The nutritionist's day-level flagging exists to catch individual outliers as they
happen, but the coach's weekly explanation is where the more useful, pattern-level read belongs —
"the week overall" is a more honest and more actionable thing to say than any single day's number.

## Spreading protein and variety across days

A week where every dinner draws from the same food family — three pasta-and-cheese dinners in a
row, say — tends to be protein-light and carb-heavy even if no single day looks dramatically
"wrong" in isolation. Spreading protein sources across the week (varying between meat, fish, egg,
and plant proteins night to night) and mixing in more vegetable-forward meals alongside heavier
ones is a more sustainable way to hit a weekly protein target than trying to force every single
dinner to be protein-maximal. Recipe `tags` and `cuisine` are a useful signal here alongside the raw
macros — a week that's nutritionally fine on paper but repeats the same handful of recipes may still
be worth a gentle "want some variety?" nudge from the coach, separate from anything nutrition-driven.

## Red flags worth mentioning gently

A few patterns are worth a coach actually raising, rather than letting slide silently:

- **Most days of the week land the same direction** (consistently under or consistently over target)
  rather than an occasional outlier day — this is the clearest signal that the current week's plan
  doesn't match the household's stated goal, not just ordinary variation.
- **Protein running low across several days in a row**, especially alongside carb- or fat-heavy
  meals — often fixable by favoring a few more protein-forward recipes in the next plan rather than
  a wholesale diet change.
- **A target that looks badly miscalibrated** — for example, totals that are off by a huge margin
  every single day, which more often means `settings` is stale (a `householdSize` that hasn't been
  updated since someone moved in or out, or a target set for a very different context) than that the
  household's eating suddenly changed dramatically. In this case the right move is suggesting the
  household double-check `settings`, not repeatedly flagging every day as an outlier.

None of these should be delivered as a verdict or a lecture — see `coaching/not-a-dietitian`'s
`plain-language-explaining.md` for how to phrase a pattern-level observation as a gentle, actionable
nudge rather than a judgment.

# Structuring a report

## Answer the actual question first

A deep dive is requested either against an existing article (implicitly: "tell me more about
this") or a free topic typed in chat (explicitly whatever the reader asked). Either way, the first
paragraph of `body` should directly address what's actually being asked, in plain terms, before any
supporting detail — a reader should be able to read just the opening and get the core answer; the
rest of the report is depth for a reader who wants it, not a mystery that unfolds only at the end.
This mirrors ordinary explanatory writing: lead with the answer, then support it, rather than
building up to a reveal.

## A useful shape, in roughly this order

1. **Direct answer / orientation** — one paragraph, what this topic actually is and what the
   headline finding is, stated plainly.
2. **Supporting detail** — the substantive middle of the report: what multiple sources actually
   say, where they agree, and where they disagree (and why, if that's discoverable — e.g. one
   source is more recent, or covers a different region/version of the story).
3. **Caveats and open questions** — what's still genuinely unsettled or unconfirmed; a report that
   presents contested or uncertain information as flatly settled is worse than one that's honest
   about the uncertainty.
4. **Sources**, cited inline throughout (see `grounding-and-honesty.md`) rather than only dumped in
   a bibliography at the end — a reader (or a later synthesizer/editor) should be able to see
   exactly which source backs which specific claim.

## Depth without padding

"More depth than the feed article" does not mean "as long as possible." A report earns its length
by including detail a reader couldn't already get from the article — a fact from a source the
synthesizer's single raw item didn't have, a clarification of a term, the actual data behind a
claim the article stated in summary form. Restating the original article's content in different
words, or padding with generic background a reader plausibly already knows, wastes the reader's
time on a request they made specifically because they wanted *more*, not *the same, longer*.
Two or three well-chosen sources synthesized honestly beat ten mediocre search results summarized
shallowly.

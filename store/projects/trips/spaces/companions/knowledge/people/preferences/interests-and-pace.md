# Interests and pace

## Interests shape what gets planned, not just what gets tolerated

An `interest`-category preference ("loves museums," "into street food," "wants a beach day") is
positive signal the planner can build the itinerary around, not just a constraint to avoid
violating. Where diet and mobility preferences are mostly about what to rule out or accommodate,
interests are about what to actively include — the host's note should surface them in a way that
reads as "here's what would make this trip good for this person," not just "here's what not to
schedule." When multiple travelers share an interest, note the overlap explicitly (it's an easy
win for a shared activity); when interests diverge sharply across the party, that's a case for
`people/group-travel`'s reconciliation guidance rather than just listing them side by side.

## Pace is about rhythm, not just speed

A `pace` preference ("slow mornings," "likes to pack the day full," "one big activity per day is
enough") describes the rhythm a traveler wants the days to have, which interacts with almost
everything else in the itinerary — a fast-paced traveler paired with an early wake-up-heavy plan
thrives; the same plan exhausts someone who prefers slow mornings. Pace preferences are usually
`weight` well under 1 (soft) unless tied to a real capacity limit (see mobility above), but "soft"
doesn't mean "ignorable" — a trip that consistently ignores a stated pace preference across every
day produces a traveler who's tired and resentful by day three, which is exactly the kind of
outcome the host's note exists to prevent.

## Recording enough detail to actually act on

An interest or pace preference recorded as a single vague word ("culture," "relaxed") is much less
useful downstream than one with a little specificity ("culture — especially local history and
small museums, not big blockbuster exhibits" or "relaxed — happy with one activity a day, prefers
not to be up before 9"). When a traveler's own words carry that detail, keep it in `notes` rather
than compressing it to the bare `value` — the planner reading the merged note benefits from the
extra texture, and the host shouldn't discard information a traveler explicitly provided just to
keep the summary shorter.
